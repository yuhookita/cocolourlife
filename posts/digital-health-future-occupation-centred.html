Digital Health and the Future of Occupation-Centred Practice

The rapid growth of digital health technologies has fundamentally changed how healthcare is delivered and experienced. Telehealth, mobile applications, wearable devices, and digital platforms are creating new possibilities for supporting people in their everyday lives, regardless of distance or location. For occupational therapy and occupation-centred practice, these technologies represent both an opportunity and a challenge.

Telehealth as a bridge

Telehealth has proven to be far more than a temporary solution during global disruptions. It has become a permanent and valued mode of service delivery that extends reach, reduces barriers, and enables more flexible and responsive care. For people in rural and remote areas, those with mobility challenges, or individuals managing complex life circumstances, telehealth can be the bridge that connects them with the support they need.

Our research into telehealth and goal-setting has highlighted how digital platforms can support collaborative and client-centred practice. When used thoughtfully, telehealth does not diminish the therapeutic relationship but can enhance it by enabling more frequent contact, real-time problem-solving, and greater integration of therapy into daily routines.

Challenges and considerations

However, the integration of digital health into occupation-centred practice is not without its challenges. Digital literacy varies widely across populations and age groups. Access to technology and reliable internet remains uneven. And the design of many digital health tools does not yet reflect the values and priorities of occupation-centred thinking.

Technology should serve the person, not the other way around. The best digital health tools are those designed with the lived experience of everyday participation at their centre.

At CoColour Life, we are committed to exploring how digital health can be used meaningfully and equitably. Through research, advisory work, and education, we are working to ensure that as the field evolves, it remains grounded in what matters most: supporting people to participate in the occupations that bring meaning and colour to their lives.

The future of occupation-centred practice is increasingly digital. Our responsibility is to shape that future in a way that is inclusive, evidence-informed, and deeply human.
